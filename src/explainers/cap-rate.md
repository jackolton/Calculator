## How it works

Cap rate — capitalization rate — is Net Operating Income divided by the property's purchase price, expressed as a percentage. NOI is annual gross rent minus operating expenses (taxes, insurance, maintenance, management fees, vacancy allowance). The purchase price is the full acquisition cost, not your equity.

The formula strips out financing on purpose. Whether you pay cash or take a 30-year mortgage doesn't change the property's income-generating ability. Cap rate measures the asset, not the deal structure.

Run it on list price when you're screening deals, then re-run it on your actual offer price. A 100K discount on a $300K property moves cap rate by roughly 1.5 points.

## When to use it

- Comparing two properties in the same market at the same price point.
- Quick screening to filter out properties before doing deeper underwriting.
- Checking whether a seller's asking price is reasonable against market cap rates.
- Tracking a portfolio's overall yield as properties are bought and sold.

Don't use cap rate across wildly different markets — a 5% cap rate in San Francisco and a 5% cap rate in Memphis are not equivalent risks.

## Common mistakes

**Using gross rent instead of NOI.** A property grossing $36K/year and a property netting $36K/year are very different. Always deduct expenses first.

**Forgetting vacancy.** Even a well-run property sits empty between tenants. A 5–10% vacancy allowance on gross rent is standard. Skipping it inflates your NOI and understates risk.

**Using list price, not all-in cost.** Closing costs, immediate repairs, and rehab all belong in the denominator. A $15K roof on day one changes the cap rate on a $200K house by nearly half a point.

**Comparing cap rates across asset classes.** A 6% cap rate on a Class A apartment building is different from a 6% cap rate on a single-family rental. Risk profiles, management intensity, and financing terms all differ.

**Treating it as a hold decision.** Cap rate is an acquisition and comparison tool. Once you own the property, cash-on-cash return and total return (including appreciation) matter more.

## Worked example

Take the defaults: $300,000 purchase price, $2,800/month gross rent, 40% expense ratio.

Annual gross rent: $2,800 × 12 = **$33,600**

Operating expenses: $33,600 × 40% = **$13,440**

NOI: $33,600 − $13,440 = **$20,160**

Cap rate: $20,160 ÷ $300,000 = **6.72%**

That's a reasonable number for a Midwest or Sun Belt market. In a coastal market, you'd typically see 3–5% on a stabilized property.
